Simulator Interpretation The simulator uses a heuristic energy-pumping policy whose quality is scaled per agent. Double DQN and REINFORCE solve the task in approximately 80–120 steps, demonstrating efficient energy injection and timely stabilisation near the goal. Tabular methods such as SARSA and Q-Learning require 200+ steps and occasionally time out, reflecting the limitations of coarse state discretisation. The difference is most visible in the early swing phase: stronger agents commit to a consistent pumping direction, while weaker agents waste steps with suboptimal or random torque applications.

Learning Curve Analysis REINFORCE and A2C converge fastest, reaching near-optimal performance within 200-300 episodes due to their direct policy optimisation and n-step TD updates respectively. Among tabular methods, SARSA(λ=0.9) converges significantly faster than both standard SARSA and Q-Learning, reaching a smoothed reward of -200 by episode 1,412 compared to 8,938 for SARSA and 9,922 for Q-Learning. Q-Learning is notably slow despite being off-policy; its smoothed reward remains below -300 for over 2,600 episodes, likely because the default [6,6,6,6,10,10] discretisation limits its ability to distinguish critical states. Double DQN achieves strong performance within its 2,000-episode training budget, benefiting from experience replay, dueling architecture, and continuous state representation.

3D Value Function Surfaces

V(s) = maxa Q(s,a) over cos(θ₁) vs θ̇₁ — drag to rotate, scroll to zoom. Notice how DQN produces smooth surfaces while tabular methods show blocky discretisation artefacts.

Value Surface Interpretation The 3D surfaces visualise V(s) = maxa Q(s,a) as a function of cos(θ₁) and angular velocity θ̇₁, with all other state dimensions held at their resting values. Double DQN produces a smooth, continuous value landscape because it operates on raw continuous states through a neural network, allowing fine-grained generalisation between nearby states. In contrast, Q-Learning and SARSA(λ) exhibit pronounced blocky artefacts corresponding to the discrete bin boundaries of the [6,6,6,6,10,10] configuration. The highest-value regions (brightest) appear where cos(θ₁) is near +1 (upright) and angular velocity is moderate, indicating the agent has learned that being near the goal with controlled momentum is the most valuable configuration.

Performance Landscape Interpretation The 3D scatter positions each agent along three evaluation axes: mean reward (higher is better), standard deviation (lower is more stable), and convergence speed measured as episodes to first reach a smoothed reward of -300. The top-right cluster containing Double DQN, REINFORCE, and A2C represents the best-performing agents, combining high reward with low variance and fast convergence. SARSA(λ) occupies a middle position with competitive final performance but slower convergence than the neural methods. The bottom-left region shows SARSA and Q-Learning, which suffer from both lower mean reward and higher variance. Notably, Double DQN achieves the tightest reward distribution (Std = 11.9) while training for only 2,000 episodes, demonstrating the sample efficiency of deep value methods with experience replay.

Results Summary Pairwise Welch's t-tests and Mann-Whitney U tests with Bonferroni correction (adjusted alpha = 0.0033) confirm that most agent pairs differ significantly. The three neural-network-based agents form a clear top tier: Double DQN (-80.2 +/- 11.9), REINFORCE (-84.7 +/- 15.6), and A2C (-87.8 +/- 28.3), with no statistically significant difference between REINFORCE and A2C (Welch-t p = 0.34). Among tabular methods, SARSA(λ=0.9) (-181.9 +/- 56.6) significantly outperforms all others. Interestingly, Q-Learning (-253.9) performs significantly worse than standard SARSA (-215.7) on this problem (p = 0.0001), suggesting that the maximisation bias is harmful under coarse discretisation. The difference between SARSA and n-step SARSA is not significant (p = 0.28). Multi-seed evaluation (10 seeds, 50 episodes each) validates these rankings with tight 95% confidence intervals, with DQN showing the smallest seed-to-seed variance (Std = 1.4).

Hyperparameter Reference

Optimal parameters found through systematic grid search for each algorithm

Hyperparameter Search Methodology Parameters were tuned via grid search with the following protocol: each candidate configuration was trained for 5,000 episodes (or 1,000 for REINFORCE), and the mean reward over the final 500 episodes was used as the selection criterion. Key findings include: (1) for Q-Learning, alpha=0.2 achieved the best mean (-285.1) while alpha=0.3 diverged (-359.7); slower epsilon decay (0.9995) was selected over the 5k-optimal 0.995 to sustain exploration across the full 15,000-episode horizon; (2) for SARSA(λ), λ=0.9 was clearly optimal (-124.2), with performance dropping sharply at λ=0 (-287.9, one-step SARSA) and with a non-monotone shape peaking at 0.9; accumulating traces (-121.4) slightly outperformed replacing traces (-137.9); (3) for n-step SARSA, n=10 was optimal (-143.2) and required a smaller alpha=0.1 (-149.0) because the compounded n-step return has higher variance; (4) the entropy coefficient β=0.01 was critical for REINFORCE and A2C, preventing premature policy collapse; and (5) soft Polyak updates (τ=0.005) provided smooth and stable training for Double DQN.
